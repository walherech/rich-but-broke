Why do physicians pay so much in taxes?

Medicine trains you to save lives. It trains you nothing about the tax code. You enter peak earning years a decade later than your peers — already in the highest brackets — with no optimization strategy in place. Without proactive above-the-line deductions and entity structuring, effective rates routinely exceed 50% in high-tax states. 82% of physicians believe they are overpaying in taxes — nearly double the rate of the general public. (Source: Physician on FIRE)

What's the difference between above-the-line and below-the-line tax strategy?

Above-the-line deductions (retirement plan contributions, HSAs, self-employment deductions) reduce your adjusted gross income before you even calculate your tax bill. For most physicians, maximizing these alone can drop an effective rate from 50%+ to approximately 40%. Below-the-line strategies — entity structuring, real estate cost segregation, bonus depreciation, charitable vehicles — are where the real transformation happens. Full optimization gets effective rates below 15%. Sometimes below 10%. This book covers both layers in sequence.

What is the Wealth Waterfall™ methodology?

The Wealth Waterfall™ is a comprehensive system for building physician wealth in the right sequence — starting with tax reduction, then asset protection, then capital deployment into private markets. It's designed to work even when you think you don't have enough capital to make meaningful moves. Kiana Danial, bestselling author and founder of Invest Diva, called it "something I rarely see: a comprehensive system to fuel true asset growth." The methodology is the backbone of this book.

How much wealth could a physician actually build through tax savings alone?

A physician earning $750,000 paying a 53% effective rate who fully optimizes their tax strategy could conservatively save $175,000 annually. Reinvested at a 7% annual yield over a 30-year career, that compounds to over $16.5 million — without changing your income, your lifestyle, or your specialty. The compounding effect of reclaiming capital early is one of the most underestimated forces in physician wealth building.
